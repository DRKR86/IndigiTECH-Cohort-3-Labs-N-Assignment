  1. Nathalie Bertin (Métis)

    2. Nathalie’s vibrant paintings often feature floral beadwork patterns and Métis cultural themes, blending contemporary and traditional elements to honor her ancestry and address issues of Indigenous resilience.

  2. Kent Monkman

    3. (Cree) – Known for his bold, large-scale paintings and multimedia installations, Kent challenges historical narratives with humor and powerful social commentary, often reimagining scenes from Canadian history through a Cree lens.

  3. Christi Belcourt (Métis)

    4. A celebrated painter, Christi’s work is rooted in Métis beadwork and floral patterns, often reflecting themes of environmental activism, Indigenous rights, and connections to the land.

  4. Rebecca Belmore (Anishinaabe)

    5. As an interdisciplinary artist, Rebecca uses performance, sculpture, and photography to explore themes of identity, survival, and the effects of colonialism on Indigenous communities.

  5. Norval Morrisseau (Anishinaabe)

    6. Known as the "Picasso of the North," Morrisseau’s work is iconic for its bright colors and use of Indigenous spiritual symbols. He pioneered the Woodlands style of painting, blending storytelling with spirituality and connection to nature.

  6. Lawrence Paul Yuxweluptun (Coast Salish and Okanagan)

    7. Through paintings and mixed media, Lawrence addresses Indigenous rights, environmental issues, and critiques colonial history, using surreal landscapes and symbols that highlight Indigenous sovereignty.

  7. Maureen Gruben (Inuvialuit)

    8. Gruben’s installations and textile works incorporate materials from her northern homeland, like seal skin and fur, to explore themes of place, tradition, and environmental change.

  8. Audie Murray (Métis)

    9. Audie’s work combines traditional Métis crafts with contemporary art practices. Her beadwork, installations, and sculptures reflect on her Métis identity, often reinterpreting Indigenous practices in a modern context
